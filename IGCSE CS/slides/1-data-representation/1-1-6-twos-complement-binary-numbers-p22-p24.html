Two’s Complement

Representing positive and negative integers

Up until now, we have assumed all binary numbers are positive integers. To allow the possibility of representing negative integers we make use of two’s complement (二进制补码). In this section we will again assume 8-bit registers are being used.

Only one minor change to the binary headings needs to be introduced here:

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} \mathbf{-128} & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \end{array}\]

  • the left-most bit is changed to a negative value
  • the new range of possible numbers is \(-128\) (\(10000000_2\)) to \(+127\) (\(01111111_2\))
  • a \(1\)-value in the left-most bit indicates a negative number and a \(0\)-value indicates a positive number

Writing Positive Binary Numbers

Positive numbers in two’s complement format

The following two examples show how we can write the following positive binary numbers in the two’s complement format: \(19\) and \(4\)

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\]

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\]

As you will notice, for positive binary numbers, it is no different to what was done in Section 1.1.2.

Converting Positive Denary Numbers

Positive denary to two’s complement format

If we wish to convert a positive denary number to the two’s complement format, we do exactly the same as in Section 1.1.2:

  • \(38\) gives 00100110
  • \(125\) gives 01111101

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\]

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\]

Converting Positive Binary Numbers

Positive two’s complement to denary

Each time a \(1\) appears in a column, the column value is added to the total:

  • 01101110 gives \(64 + 32 + 8 + 4 + 2 = 110\)
  • 00111111 gives \(32 + 16 + 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 = 63\)

Reading Negative Two’s Complement

Interpreting numbers with MSB \(= 1\)

Each time a \(1\) appears in a column, the column value is added to the total:

Example: 10010011

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\]

\(-128 + 16 + 2 + 1 = \mathbf{-109}\)

Example: 11100100

\(-128 + 64 + 32 + 4 = \mathbf{-28}\)

Example: 11110101

\(-128 + 64 + 32 + 16 + 4 + 1 = \mathbf{-11}\)

A two’s complement number with a \(1\)-value in the \(-128\) column must represent a negative binary number.

Converting Negative Denary Numbers

Method 1 and Method 2

Consider the number \(+67\) in 8-bit two’s complement binary format:

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\]

Method 1: put \(1\)s in their correct places for \(-67\)

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\]

since \(-128 + 32 + 16 + 8 + 4 + 1 = -67\)

Method 2: first write the number as a positive binary value, then invert and add \(1\)

 67  = 01000011
invert -> 10111100
+1     -> 10111101

Worked Example: \(-79\)

Convert \(-79\) into 8-bit two’s complement

Method 1

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c} -128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\]

because \(-79 = -128 + 49\) and \(49 = 32 + 16 + 1\).

Method 2

 79     = 01001111
invert  = 10110000
+1      = 10110001

Both methods give 10110001. It is a good idea to practise both methods.

Not Limited to 8 Bits

Two’s complement with fewer bits

When applying two’s complement, it isn’t always necessary for a binary number to have \(8\) bits:

\[\begin{array}{c c c c} -8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\]

Applying two’s complement (1001 + 1) would give:

\[\begin{array}{c c c c} -8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\]

In other words: \(-6\)

Example with 12 Bits

Converting \(-1676\)

The following \(12\)-bit binary number represents denary number \(1676\):

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c c c c c} -2048 & 1024 & 512 & 256 & 128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\]

Applying two’s complement (100101110011 + 1) would give:

\[\begin{array}{c c c c c c c c c c c c} -2048 & 1024 & 512 & 256 & 128 & 64 & 32 & 16 & 8 & 4 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\]

In other words: \(-1676\)

Classroom Check

Understand two’s complement representation

A complete answer should include:

  • that two’s complement allows representation of both positive and negative integers in binary
  • that the left-most heading changes from \(+128\) to \(-128\) in an 8-bit register
  • that a \(0\)-value in the left-most bit indicates a positive number and a \(1\)-value indicates a negative number
  • that positive numbers are written in the same way as ordinary binary, provided the left-most bit is \(0\)
  • the two methods for negative numbers: direct placement using the negative left-most column, or invert all bits and add \(1\)
  • the 8-bit two’s complement range: \(-128\) to \(+127\)
